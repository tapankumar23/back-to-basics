01 Reliability — continuing to work correctly even when things go wrong

DDIA distinguishes faults (one component deviating from its spec) from failures (the whole system stops). Fault-tolerant systems anticipate faults and prevent them from causing failures. Three categories:

HW Hardware Faults

Disks crash, RAM fails, power cuts out. RAID arrays reconstruct data from remaining disks.

Ready — click Inject Fault
SW Software Faults

Runaway processes, cascading failures, bugs triggered by unusual inputs.

5-node cluster — stable
HUMAN Human Errors

Config mistakes cause more outages than hardware. Canary deployments limit blast radius.

04 Maintainability — Operability, Simplicity, and Evolvability

The majority of software cost is in ongoing maintenance: fixing bugs, adapting to new platforms, repaying technical debt, and adding new features. DDIA identifies three design principles.

Operability

Make it easy for ops teams to keep the system running smoothly.

  • ✓ Monitoring & observability
  • ✓ Automated deployment
  • ✓ Runbooks & documentation
  • ✓ Self-healing & predictable behavior

Simplicity

Remove accidental complexity; keep essential complexity explicit.

Evolvability

Design for easy future changes; prefer backward-compatible evolution.
